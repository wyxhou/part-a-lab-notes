EXP 1 16 Personality Factor Questionnaire (16PF) ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess the personality of the subject by administering 16 PF Form 'C'.

Devised by Raymond B. Cattell. An objectively scorable test that gives the most complete coverage of personality in a short time. Designed for literate individuals aged 16 and above whose educational level is roughly equivalent to that of the normal high school student.

The test covers 16 dimensions of personality — functionally independent and psychologically meaningful.
Form C has 105 items (8 items per factor for most, 6 for remaining scales, 7 for Factor B). Also has a motivational distortion scale and a distortion scale to minimise faking.
Plan
To administer the questionnaire to the subject and score the responses with the help of the key and interpret the scores using the norms.
Materials
16 PF Form 'C' questionnaire Answer Sheet / Data Sheet Key Norms Writing Materials
Precautions
  1. The subject is asked not to waste time pondering over a question.
  2. The subject should not leave any question unanswered.
  3. The subject should avoid giving the 'middle' answer as far as possible.
  4. The subject should be encouraged to answer as honestly as possible.
Procedure
  1. Seat the subject comfortably and establish rapport. Present the 16 PF questionnaire and data sheet and read the instructions.
  2. There is no time limit. In the end scoring will be done with the help of the key and the results will be interpreted with the help of the norms.
Instructions
"There are some questions to see what attitudes and interests you have. There are no right or wrong answers because every one has a right to their views. Answer them honestly in a separate answer sheet. There are three possible answers to each question. Fill in the left box if 'a', the middle box if 'b', right box if 'c'. Some may not apply to you but guess. Answers will be kept confidential, so do not hesitate to answer. Some may seem personal."
Scoring & Analysis
  1. Score the subject's responses with the help of the key. Each answer scores 0, 1, or 2 points, except for Factor B (Intelligence) which uses a correct/incorrect format.
  2. Find out the total raw score for each of the 16 factors separately using the scoring key.
  3. Convert each raw score into a sten score (Standard Ten) using the population norms (normal adults, college students etc.). Sten 4–7 = Average · 8–10 = High · 1–3 = Low.
  4. MD Score check: If the M.D. sten score is 7 and above, corrections must be made to the remaining sten scores per the manual. A low MD score indicates the subject answered genuinely — no corrections needed.
  5. Plot all sten scores on a graph to obtain a personality profile. Interpret each factor separately and assess the overall personality of the subject.
Sten Norms (Standard Ten): Sten 4–7 = Average · 8–10 = High · 1–3 = Low.
⚠ If MD sten ≥ 7, apply corrections to remaining scores as per the manual.

EXP 2 Assessment of Anxiety ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess the level of anxiety through Anxiety Scale Self Analysis Form.

Anxiety has been defined as a "state of arousal" caused by threat to the well-being (Spielberg 1960). "State" = condition involving the entire organism. "Arousal" = condition of tension/unrest. "Threat" = anticipation of pain or danger.

Anxiety manifests in three ways: (a) Through a conscious state of discomfort and apprehension; (b) Through visceral disturbances especially of digestive and circulatory systems; (c) Through motor symptoms — restlessness, trembling, irritability and the like.
Scale: Anxiety Scale developed by Krug, Scheier and R.B. Cattell, first published in 1957. Includes the best 40 anxiety items among several thousand personality items. Updated in 1976 for language changes.
Sten 4–7 = average anxiety · Sten 1–3 = unusually relaxed/phlegmatic · Sten 8 = getting serious · Sten 9–10 = serious (found in 1 in 20 cases).
Plan
To administer the anxiety scale to the subject, score the responses and interpret the scores.
Materials
Anxiety Scale Questionnaire (Krug, Scheier & R.B. Cattell) Key and Norms Writing Materials
Precautions
  1. Effort should be made to secure the true answer from the subject.
  2. If the subject fails to understand the meaning of any question, the experimenter should explain it.
  3. All questions should be answered and there has to be only one answer for each question.
Procedure
  1. Seat the subject comfortably. Establish rapport. Give a copy of the anxiety scale booklet. The subject is asked to answer the questions in the scale.
  2. The responses are scored with the help of the key and the anxiety level is determined. The test is not timed but typically requires only about 10 minutes for a subject of average reading ability who is not excessively disturbed. Anyone taking an unusually long time should be reminded that the 'first response' to each question is what is needed.
Instructions
"Inside this booklet there are 40 statements about how most people feel or think at one time or another. There are no right or wrong answers. Just pick the one that is really true for you and mark 'a', 'b' or 'c'."
Analysis of Data
  1. Score the subject's answers using the key.
  2. Scores have to be added for A and B separately:

    (A) Covert – Unrealized Anxiety — subject is unaware of the symptoms of anxiety.
    (B) Overt – Symptomatic Conscious Anxiety — manifest anxiety where subject is aware of their anxiousness.
  3. Total the A and B scores (A + B = Total Score). Use the total to determine the sten score and percentile from the norms and interpret the level of anxiety.
Sten Interpretation:
Sten 4–7 = Average level of anxiety
Sten 1–3 = Unusually relaxed, secure and phlegmatic
Sten 8 = Anxiety level getting serious
Sten 9–10 = Serious anxiety level (found in only about 1 in 20 cases)

EXP 3 General Health Questionnaire (GHQ) ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess presence of Psychological Morbidity in the subject.

Scaled GHQ is a 28-item questionnaire, a modified version of GHQ of Goldberg and Hillier (1972), used as a first stage screening method for the general population.

Four sub-tests: (a) Somatic Symptoms, (b) Anxiety and Insomnia, (c) Social Dysfunction, (d) Severe Depression.
Quick method to identify "Possible Psychiatric cases" in the community. Emphasis on the "here and now" — subjects are asked about present and recent complaints, not those they had in the past.
Plan
Administer the GHQ and find out the total score of the subject and interpret with reference to the norms.
Materials
General Health Questionnaire (GHQ) Manual and Scoring Key Writing Materials
Procedure
  1. Seat the subject comfortably and give a copy of GHQ. Give the instructions to the subject.
  2. The questionnaire is usually completed in 10 to 15 minutes.
  3. After the subject completes the questionnaire, the experimenter scores the responses under the four problem areas. Each statement has 4 alternative choices (a, b, c, d).
Instructions
"I would like to know if you had any medical complaint and how your health has been in general over the past few weeks. Answer questions simply by putting a tick mark (✓) for the alternative which you think most applies to you. Remember that I want to know about present and recent complaints, and not those that you had in the past. It is important that you try to answer all the questions."
Analysis of Results
  1. A score of zero (0) is given to the first two answers (a and b) and a score of one (1) is given to the remaining two answers (c and d).
  2. The number of items identified by the subject is found out. Add the raw scores in all four dimensions (Somatic Symptoms + Anxiety & Insomnia + Social Dysfunction + Severe Depression).
  3. Individuals with a total score of 5 and above are considered to be possible cases of psychological morbidity — i.e. likely to become possible psychiatric cases if symptoms continue unabated.
Cut-off score: 5 and above = Possible Psychiatric Case / Psychological Morbidity present.
Score below 5 = Not a possible case of psychological morbidity.

EXP 4 Comprehensive Interest Schedule (CIS) ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess subject's relative interest in occupational areas using the Comprehensive Interest Schedule.

Developed by Sanjay Vohra. Useful for counselling students about career choices; also applicable to adults for selection and placement in industrial settings. Represents relative interest in 8 broad vocational areas and 14 occupational interests.

Interest has more to do with whether a task is attempted with motivation rather than how well it is performed. Interest ratings are better indices of job persistence rather than job success.
Plan
To obtain the subject's preferences for various occupations, score the preferences, convert the scores into sten scores which are interpreted, and used to draw the subject's interest profile for 14 occupational interests.
Materials
CIS form by Sanjay Vohra Norms Writing materials
Precautions
  1. Instructions to the subject should be clear, to enable the subject to mark their preferences accurately.
  2. The subject should be encouraged to respond honestly, and to consider the occupations irrespective of the income or status involved.
  3. Ask the subject to work rapidly.
Procedure
  1. Seat subject comfortably and place the CIS form in front of the subject. Using the examples on the first page, instruct the subject.
  2. Occupations are given in pairs. The subject draws a circle around the number of the occupation they prefer in each pair. They may choose one, both or none. Do not consider income or status — only the work involved.
  3. When the subject has understood the instructions, ask them to open the schedule and mark their preferences. There is no time limit, but it should be pointed out that results will probably be better if they work rapidly.
  4. After the subject has finished responding, score the preferences, convert into sten scores and draw the subject's interest profile.
Sten score interpretation: 8–10 = High Interest · 4–7 = Average Interest · 1–3 = Low Interest
Instructions
"You have to mark your preference for different occupations in this schedule. These occupations are given in pairs and you have to indicate your preference by circling a number. You can circle both items in a pair if you like both, otherwise circle one. If you dislike both you can cross out both numbers. Mark your preferences without considering the income or status involved in each occupation. There is no time limit, but work quickly as your first impression is more likely to give a true picture of your likes and dislikes."
Analysis
  1. Count the number of circles around 'ones' in each of the columns and enter the number at the bottom of each column.
  2. Count the number of circles around 'twos' in each of the rows and enter the number at the side of each column.
  3. Transfer those scores onto the CIS Profile sheet. Obtain the total raw score for each occupation by adding the scores for 'ones' and 'twos'. The maximum raw score possible for each field is 28.
  4. Using the norms, convert the raw scores into sten scores and interpret the subject's interest level in each of the 14 occupational fields:

    8–10 = High Interest  ·  4–7 = Average Interest  ·  1–3 = Low Interest
  5. The sten scores are used to draw the subject's Interest Profile across all 14 occupational fields.
Sten Score Interpretation: 8–10 = High Interest · 4–7 = Average Interest · 1–3 = Low Interest
Maximum raw score per field = 28. There is no time limit for the CIS.
EXP 5 Emotional Maturity Scale (EMS) ▾
Problem / Aim
To assess the emotional maturity of the subject using the Emotional Maturity Scale.

Emotional maturity is a state of having reached an adult level of emotional development which implies emotional control in social situations.

According to Walter D. Smitson (1974), emotional maturity is a process in which the personality is continuously striving for greater sense of emotional health, both intra-psychically and inter-personally.

According to Cote (1944) the most outstanding mark of emotional maturity is the ability to bear tension.
Kaplan and Baron: an emotionally mature person (a) can withstand delay in satisfaction of needs (delayed gratification); (b) tolerates frustration; (c) believes in long-term planning; (d) can delay or revise expectations as the situation demands.
Scale developed by Dr. Yashvir Singh and Dr. Mahesh Bhargava. Assesses emotional maturity across 5 areas: (A) Emotional Stability, (B) Emotional Progression, (C) Social Adjustment, (D) Personality Integration, (E) Independence.
ScaleWhat it means
A – Emotional StabilityDoes not react excessively to changes in moods. Instability = quick mood changes, irritability, temper tantrums, lack of capacity to solve problems.
B – Emotional ProgressionGrowing vitality and adequacy. Regression = inferiority, restlessness, hostility, aggressiveness, self-centeredness.
C – Social AdjustmentIntegration between personal needs and social environment demands. Poor adjustment = lack of adaptability, hatred, seclusion, shrinking responsibility.
D – Personality IntegrationFirmly unifying diverse elements of one's motives. Disintegration = phobia, pessimism, immorality, distorted sense of reality.
E – IndependenceCapacity to be self-reliant using intellectual and creative potentialities. Dependent person shows parasitic dependence on others.
Plan
Administer Emotional Maturity Scale. Score the responses of the subject, find out the total score. Interpret the result using the norms.
Materials
Emotional Maturity Scale Norms and Key Writing materials
Precautions
  1. The experimenter should make sure that the subject has understood the instructions.
  2. The subject is encouraged to be honest and spontaneous while giving the responses.
  3. The subject should interpret the questions by himself. The experimenter may give the meaning of the question.
  4. The subject must be assured about the confidentiality of the test results.
Procedure
  1. Seat the subject comfortably and give them a copy of the Emotional Maturity Scale including the personal data.
  2. Give the instructions are given to the subject. The experimenter can help the subject understand the meaning of difficult words. Takes around 15–20 minutes to complete.
  3. After the subject completes the test, the responses are scored and the total score is compared to the norms to determine the emotional stability of the subject.
Total score interpretation: 50–80 = Extremely stable · 81–88 = Moderately stable · 89–106 = Unstable · 107–240 = Extremely unstable
Instructions
"In the following pages there are forty eight statements about yourself. Five possible modes of responses are provided, such as 'Very Much', 'Much', 'Undecided', 'probably' and 'Never'. Read each question carefully and mark tick in any one of the five alternative response modes to indicate your level of agreement with the particular content of the statement. Do not think too much while answering. Your responses will be kept confidential. There are no right or wrong answers, so answer honestly and spontaneously. Try and respond to all statements. Indicate whatever you may feel."
Analysis of Results
  1. The responses of the subject are scored as follows:

    Very much = 5  ·  Much = 4  ·  Undecided = 3  ·  Probably = 2  ·  Never = 1
  2. Find out the total score of the subject by adding all responses. Refer to the norms and interpret accordingly using the Total Score table (a) below.
  3. Also find out the score on each of the five sub-scales (A, B, C, D, E) separately and interpret each using the Sub-scale norms table (b) below.
(a) Total Score Interpretation:
50–80 = Extremely Stable  ·  81–88 = Moderately Stable  ·  89–106 = Unstable  ·  107–240 = Extremely Unstable

(b) Sub-scale Score Interpretation — Scales A, B, C, D:
10–16 = Extremely Stable  ·  17–19 = Moderately Stable  ·  20–32 = Unstable  ·  33–50 = Extremely Unstable

(c) Sub-scale Score Interpretation — Scale E (Independence):
8–13 = Extremely Stable  ·  14–16 = Moderately Stable  ·  17–25 = Unstable  ·  26–40 = Extremely Unstable

EXP 1 16PF — Introduction ▾
About the Test

16PF was devised by Raymond B. Cattell. It is an objectively scorable test which gives the most complete coverage of personality in a brief time. Designed for use with literate individuals aged 16 years and above whose educational level is roughly equivalent to that of the normal high school student.

The correlation among the 16 scales is generally quite small, so each scale provides some new piece of information about the person being tested. The 16 dimensions are not just unique to the test but instead rest within the context of a general theory of personality.

Sixteen functionally independent and psychologically meaningful dimensions measured by 16PF give a comprehensive coverage of personality. They have been isolated and replicated in more than 30 years of factor analytic research on normal and clinical groups.

About Form C

The present test is Form C of 16PF, which has 8 items for factor B scale, 7 items for factor A scale, and 6 items for the remaining scales. The questions are arranged in a roughly cyclic order determined by a plan to give maximum convenience in scoring by stencil and to ensure variety and interest.

Form C is very frequently used in occupational selection, counselling and therapy.

The test has an Affectothymia accompanied by a brief, less technical title like Schizothymia versus cool, warm hearted, easy going and participating. Each of the primary factors measured by 16PF has an alphabetical design (A through Q4) with a technical title.
A motivational distortion scale is aimed at minimising distortion and deliberate faking. Rapport is a step towards achieving a good measure — accurate results contribute to the subject's better understanding of careful and truthful responses.
Scoring & Norms

The standardisation tables convert raw scores to what are called 'stens' (Standard Ten). Sten scores are distributed from 1 through 10. Sten scores of 4, 5, 6 and 7 indicate average; sten scores of 8, 9 and 10 indicate high scores; sten scores of 1, 2 and 3 indicate low scores.

Raw score must be converted into a sten score which places the subject's scores in relation to scores obtained by other people in the defined population (normal adults, college students etc.). If the M.D. sten score is 7 and above, make corrections to the remaining sten scores according to the manual.

EXP 2 Anxiety — Introduction ▾
Definition

Anxiety has been defined as a "state of arousal" caused by threat to the well-being (Spielberg 1960).

  • "State" means a condition involving the entire organism.
  • "Arousal" means a condition of tension, unrest or uneasiness or readiness to act and respond.
  • "Threat" means anticipation of pain or danger or serious interference with goal seeking activities.
Three Ways Anxiety Manifests
(a) Through a conscious state of discomfort and apprehension.
(b) Through visceral disturbances especially of digestive and circulatory systems.
(c) Through motor symptoms which include restlessness, trembling, irritability and the like.
About the Scale

One of the scales used frequently for the assessment of anxiety is the Anxiety Scale developed by Krug, Scheier and R.B. Cattell, first published in 1957. It includes the best 40 anxiety items among several thousand personality items. In 1976 items in the test were carefully updated to adjust for language changes which had taken place.

Generally a sten score of 4 to 7 indicates an average level of anxiety. Sten scores of 1 to 3 are typically found in unusually relaxed, secure and phlegmatic individuals. A sten score of 8 indicates a person whose anxiety level would be getting serious while stens of 9 to 10 are found in only about 1 in 20 cases.
Covert vs. Overt Anxiety

Scores have to be added for A and B separately:

(A) Covert – Unrealized Anxiety — where the subject is unaware of the symptoms of anxiety.
(B) Overt – Symptomatic conscious anxiety — or manifest anxiety where the subject is aware of their anxiousness (currently seen).

EXP 3 GHQ — Introduction ▾
About the GHQ

The presence and severity of psychological symptoms can be assessed through the GHQ. Scaled GHQ is a 28-item questionnaire, which is a modified version of GHQ of Goldberg and Hillier (1972) and has been used as a first stage screening method for the general population. The scaled version of the GHQ is intended for use in studies in which an investigator requires more information than is provided by a single severity score.

Four Sub-tests
Scale GHQ consists of four sub-tests: (a) Somatic Symptoms, (b) Anxiety and Insomnia, (c) Social Dysfunction, (d) Severe Depression.
Purpose & Focus

It is a quick and easy method to identify "Possible Psychiatric cases" in the community. As a screening device, it gives reliable information as a first stage screening process in community mental health services.

Emphasis is on the "here and now". Subjects are asked to respond to the questions in terms of their present and recent complaints, not those that they had in the past.

In the Indian condition, GHQ has proved to be a reliable and valid tool in population surveys.

Scoring

A score of zero is given to the first two answers (a and b) and a score of one is given to the remaining two answers (c and d). Individuals with a total score of 5 and above are considered to be possible cases of psychological morbidity — i.e. likely to become possible psychiatric cases if symptoms continue unabated.

EXP 4 CIS — Introduction ▾
About Interest

The strength and direction of the individual's interests represents an important aspect of their personality and affects educational and vocational pursuits. Interest has more to do with whether a task is attempted with motivation rather than how well it is performed. One's performance on the job depends on abilities. However, whether or not they stay on the job will largely reflect whether they like or dislike it. For this reason, interest ratings are better indices of job persistence rather than of job success.

The study of interest has received its strongest impetus from vocational educational counselling.

About the CIS

The Comprehensive Interest Schedule, by Sanjay Vohra, is useful for counselling high school and college students about their career choices, and is also applicable to adults for selection and placement in industrial settings.

The CIS is an objective device representing relative interest in eight broad vocational areas, and fourteen occupational interests.

The 8 broad vocational areas: Influential · Venturous · Artistic · Scientific · Analytical · Social · Nature · Clerical
Scoring

The maximum raw score possible for each field is 28. Using the norms, raw scores are converted into sten scores.

8–10 = High Interest · 4–7 = Average Interest · 1–3 = Low Interest

The sten scores are used to draw the subject's Interest profile.

Viva Q&A
What does the CIS measure and who developed it? EXP 4
The Comprehensive Interest Schedule was developed by Sanjay Vohra. It measures relative interest in 8 broad vocational areas and 14 occupational interests. Useful for career counselling with students and for selection/placement in industrial settings.
Why are interest ratings better indices of job persistence than job success? EXP 4

EXP 5 EMS — Introduction ▾
Definition

Emotional maturity is a state of having reached an adult level of emotional development which implies emotional control in social situations.

According to Walter D. Smitson (1974), emotional maturity is a process in which the personality is continuously striving for greater sense of emotional health, both intra-psychically and inter-personally.

According to Cote (1944), the most outstanding mark (make) of emotional maturity is the ability to bear tension.
Characteristics of an Emotionally Mature Person

Kaplan and Baron elaborate the characteristics of an emotionally mature person. He/She:

(a) Has the capacity to withstand delay in satisfaction of needs — delayed gratification.
(b) Ability to tolerate a reasonable amount of frustration.
(c) Belief in long term planning.
(d) Capable of delaying or revising expectations in terms of the demands the situation makes.

An emotionally mature individual is not the one who has resolved anxiety and hostility, but is continuously in a process of seeing himself in a clear perspective and is involved in a struggle to gain healthy integration of feeling, thinking and action.

An emotionally immature person reacts like a child, looks for sympathy, is conceited, infantile, self-centered, demanding, emotionally excited and is very upset to lose a game.

The 5 Broad Factors

The authors of the Emotional Maturity Scale prepared a list of five broad factors of emotional maturity:

I. Emotional Stability — refers to the characteristics of a person that does not allow them to react excessively to changes in moods or emotive situations. The emotionally stable person is able to do what is required in any given situation. Emotional instability is a tendency for quick changes of emotions with irritability, stubbornness, temper tantrums, lack of capacity to solve problems and seeking help from others for day to day problems.
II. Emotional Progression — the characteristic of a person that refers to a feeling of adequacy and growing vitality of emotions in relation to the environment. Emotional regression consists of syndromes like feeling of inferiority, restlessness, hostility, aggressiveness and self-centeredness.
III. Social Adjustment — refers to a process of integration between the needs of a person and demands of the social environment, so that they can maintain and adapt a desired relationship with the environment. A socially maladjusted person shows lack of social adaptability, shows hatred, is seclusive, boasts, lies and shirks responsibility.
IV. Personality Integration — is the process of firmly unifying the diverse elements of an individual's motives and dynamic tendencies resulting in harmonious interactions. Disintegrated personality includes symptoms like phobia, rationalization, immorality, pessimism, destruction, has a distorted sense of reality and shows a varied degree of neuroticism.
V. Independence — is the capacity of a person to be self-reliant characterised by an ability to take decisions using intellectual and creative potentialities. A dependent person shows parasitic dependence on others.
Scoring

(a) Total Score Interpretation:
50–80 = Extremely Stable  ·  81–88 = Moderately Stable  ·  89–106 = Unstable  ·  107–240 = Extremely Unstable

(b) Sub-scale Interpretation — Scales A, B, C, D:
10–16 = Extremely Stable  ·  17–19 = Moderately Stable  ·  20–32 = Unstable  ·  33–50 = Extremely Unstable

(c) Sub-scale Interpretation — Scale E (Independence):
8–13 = Extremely Stable  ·  14–16 = Moderately Stable  ·  17–25 = Unstable  ·  26–40 = Extremely Unstable

STATS Non-Parametric Methods — Chi Square (χ²) ▾
When to use Non-Parametric Methods

When N is small or data is badly skewed, the assumption of normality is doubtful and parametric methods are of dubious value. Non-parametric (distribution-free) methods allow us to compare samples and make inferences without having to assume normality.

Chi Square

The Chi-square (χ²) test represents a useful method of comparing experimentally obtained results with those to be expected theoretically on some hypothesis.

Chi-Square Formula
χ² = Σ [ (fo - fe)² / fe ]
fo = frequency of observed or experimentally determined facts
fe = expected frequency of occurrence on some hypothesis
How it works
  1. Differences between observed and expected frequencies are squared and divided by the expected number in each case.
  2. Sum of these quotients = χ². Smaller χ² = observed results closer to expected.
  3. Larger χ² = greater probability of real divergence from expected results.
  4. To evaluate, enter Table E with computed χ² and degrees of freedom.
  5. df = (r-1)(c-1) where r = number of rows, c = number of columns.
Interpretation Rules
If chi-square value is greater than table value → null hypothesis is rejected, but significance of trend is present.
If chi-square value is lesser than table value → null hypothesis is accepted, no significant trend indicated.
If chi-square value is between 0.05 and 0.01 table values → hypothesis rejected at 0.05 level, but at 0.01 level accepted.
Worked Example (Part A — Stats)

75 subjects were asked — "Should dresscode be enforced in college?" 50 answered No, 15 answered Yes, 10 Undecided. Does the result indicate a significant trend of opinion?

Textbook Example (reference)

48 subjects asked about joining a Nations organisation for control of atomic power — 24 Favourable, 12 Indifferent, 12 Unfavourable. χ² computed = 6. With df = 2, table values: 0.05 = 5.991, 0.01 = 9.21. The obtained χ² of 6 is greater than 5.990 but less than 9.21. Hence the null hypothesis is rejected at the 0.05 level and not at the 0.01 level.
